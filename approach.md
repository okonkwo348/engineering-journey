# Engineering Growth Protocol

**Author:** My Mentor (ChatGPT)

**Purpose:** A long-term guide for becoming an exceptional software engineer.

> *"Do not optimize for finishing projects. Optimize for becoming the engineer who can build any project."*

---

# Part I — My Strengths

These are habits I should protect because they accelerate my growth.

## 1. I Ask "Why?"

I naturally ask questions about:

* Why something works.
* Why one design is better than another.
* Why responsibilities belong in certain places.

This curiosity is one of my greatest assets.

**Rule:** Never lose the habit of asking "Why?"

---

## 2. I Persist Through Difficulty

I don't give up easily.

When something is confusing, I continue asking questions until I understand the underlying concept.

**Rule:** Keep embracing productive struggle.

---

## 3. I Learn Through Refactoring

I don't just accept working code.

Once I understand a solution, I become interested in improving its design.

This is how engineers grow beyond programming.

---

## 4. I Care About Understanding

I prefer understanding over copying.

This is a long-term advantage.

---

# Part II — My Growth Areas

These are not weaknesses.

They are the next skills I must deliberately develop.

---

## Growth Area 1

### Stop Solving Today's Problem

I sometimes design only for the current requirement.

Professional engineers design for change.

Before implementing a solution, ask:

* What if another feature is added?
* What if this code needs to be reused?
* What if requirements change tomorrow?

---

## Growth Area 2

### Model Before Building

Sometimes I think about folders, handlers, and files before fully understanding the system.

New protocol:

Before writing code, define:

* Inputs
* Outputs
* Transformations
* Responsibilities

Only then design the architecture.

---

## Growth Area 3

### Think in Responsibilities, Not Files

Instead of asking:

> Which file should this go in?

Ask:

> Who owns this responsibility?

Files are implementation details.

Responsibilities define architecture.

---

## Growth Area 4

### Challenge My Own Designs

I already challenge my mentor's suggestions.

I should also challenge my own.

Ask:

* Can this be simpler?
* Can this be reused?
* Is there unnecessary duplication?
* Am I making assumptions?

Become my own first code reviewer.

---

## Growth Area 5

### Build Stronger Abstractions

Move beyond project-specific thinking.

Example:

Don't think:

> prepareASCII()

Think:

> Shared business logic.

Look for patterns that apply beyond the current project.

---

## Growth Area 6

### Reflect Before Moving On

I sometimes ask:

> Are we done?

Instead, ask:

* Why did this design work?
* What would I change if I started over?
* What was the most important engineering lesson?

Reflection converts experience into knowledge.

---

## Growth Area 7

### Trust My Reasoning

Sometimes I reach the correct conclusion but immediately seek confirmation.

Instead:

* Finish my reasoning.
* Defend it.
* Then seek review.

Confidence should come from understanding, not approval.

---

## Growth Area 8

### Read More Code

I build a lot.

I should also read.

Weekly:

* Go standard library
* High-quality open-source Go projects
* Other engineers' architecture

Great engineers read almost as much code as they write.

---

## Growth Area 9

### Improve Engineering Communication

Instead of describing files:

> "handler, templates, static"

Describe systems:

> "HTTP layer, presentation layer, static assets."

Think like an architect when explaining designs.

---

# Part III — My Engineering Protocol

Before writing any function:

1. What responsibility does this function have?
2. What information does it actually need?
3. What should it return?
4. Who should handle the error?
5. Can another part of the system reuse it?

If I cannot answer these questions, I should not write the function yet.

---

# Part IV — My Project Protocol

For every project:

## Phase 1

Understand requirements.

Never skip this.

---

## Phase 2

Model the system.

Define:

* Inputs
* Outputs
* Transformations
* Responsibilities

---

## Phase 3

Design architecture.

Only now decide:

* folders
* packages
* handlers
* models

---

## Phase 4

Build incrementally.

Small working steps.

---

## Phase 5

Refactor.

Working code is Version 1.

Good architecture comes afterward.

---

## Phase 6

Reflect.

Answer:

* What did I learn?
* What surprised me?
* What would I redesign?

---

# Part V — Rules I Want to Live By

* Simplicity beats cleverness.
* Design before implementation.
* Readability beats brevity.
* Working code is the beginning, not the end.
* Refactoring is part of development.
* Every function should have one clear responsibility.
* Every project should teach me a reusable engineering principle.
* Never stop asking "Why?"

---

# Long-Term Goal

I do not want to become someone who merely knows Go.

I want to become an engineer who can:

* Understand unfamiliar systems.
* Design maintainable software.
* Defend architectural decisions.
* Learn new technologies quickly.
* Solve problems independently.

Programming languages will change.

Engineering thinking will remain.

---

# Final Reminder

When I feel stuck, I should remember:

> "My job is not to write code.

> My job is to design systems that happen to be implemented in code."

That mindset is what separates software engineers from programmers.
